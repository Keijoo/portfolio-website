About VR & AR

Virtual Reality (VR) is a computer-generated simulation that immerses users in a fully virtual environment, while Augmented Reality (AR) overlays digital content in the real world. Both technologies are enhancing human experiences in remarkable ways.

From the early days of VR in the 1960s to today’s immersive gaming experiences, both VR and AR have come a long way. These technologies are now revolutionizing industries like healthcare, where surgeons practice with AR-assisted models, and retail, where customers can try virtual products before making a purchase.

How much does a VR headset cost?

The price of a VR headset can vary widely depending on the brand, features, and the platform it's compatible with. Here’s a rough breakdown:

  • Entry-Level VR Headsets: These typically cost between €100 and €300. These headsets may rely on smartphones or computers for processing power. Examples include: Oculus Go and Samsung Gear VR.
  • Mid-Range VR Headsets: Ranging from €300 to €600, these headsets offer better resolution, tracking, and a more immersive experience. Examples include: Quest 2 and PlayStation VR.
  • High-End VR Headsets: Prices for top-tier headsets can go from €600 to over €1,000. These headsets require a powerful PC or gaming console to work. Examples include: Valve Index and Oculus Rift S.

Note: The prices often fluctuate based on sales, promotions, or new product releases.

What are the best VR games?

The world of VR gaming is vast, with games spanning various genres. Here are some of the best VR games across different categories:

  • Action/Adventure: Half-Life: Alyx, The Walking Dead: Saints & Sinners
  • Sports/Exercise: Beat Saber, Superhot VR
  • Exploration: No Man's Sky VR, Star Trek: Bridge Crew

Note: Some games are exclusive to certain platforms, so check compatibility before purchasing.

How does AR work on mobile devices?

Augmented Reality (AR) on mobile devices works by overlaying digital information (such as images, videos, or 3D objects) on top of the real world. Here's a breakdown:

  • Camera and Sensors: Mobile devices use their built-in cameras to capture the physical environment around the user. In addition, devices have gyroscopes, accelerometers, and GPS to understand the device’s orientation, movement, and location.
  • Software and Processing: The device uses software like ARKit (iOS) or ARCore (Android) to process real-world data and accurately place virtual objects.
  • Rendering and Display: After processing, the device overlays the digital content on the camera feed, giving the illusion that virtual objects are interacting with the real world.
  • Interaction: Users can interact with these objects via touch controls, gestures, or device movement.

Example of AR apps include Pokémon GO, IKEA Place, and Google Lens.
